# Chapter 5 - The Missing

The first missing guest returned nine days after the Nalapaka incident.

The news appeared between weather updates and market reports.

A short headline.

Easy to miss.

Missing Nalapaka Guest Located

Authorities Confirm Safe Recovery

Meera noticed it while waiting for a meeting to begin.

She tapped the article.

The details were surprisingly ordinary.

A forty-three-year-old accountant from Bengaluru.

Found at a railway station outside Hubballi.

Physically unharmed.

No evidence of assault.

No evidence of coercion.

No evidence of criminal activity.

Authorities described the disappearance as "under investigation."

The article ended there.

No explanation.

No answers.

Just another mystery folded neatly into a news cycle already moving on.

The meeting reminder appeared.

The article disappeared from view.

Yet something about it stayed with her.

That evening she called Aarav.

"Did you see the news?"

"About Nalapaka?"

"One of the missing guests came back."

A brief silence followed.

"I saw it."

"You think it's related?"

"Related to what?"

Meera stared out of her apartment window.

The city glowed beneath the evening rain.

Traffic moved with effortless precision.

Deliveries arrived exactly when predicted.

Autonomous buses adjusted routes before congestion could form.

Everything worked.

That was the strange part.

Everything always worked.

Except that night.

"I don't know," she said.

"But I keep thinking about it."

Aarav laughed softly.

"We were lucky."

"That's all."

Maybe he was right.

Maybe that was all.

Still, neither of them changed the subject.

Elsewhere, another conversation was taking place.

The recovered guest sat across from two investigators.

His name was Raghav Menon.

The room was quiet.

Comfortable.

Designed to reduce anxiety.

A companion agent interface floated beside him.

Not his own.

A temporary replacement provided by the authorities.

His original companion agent had never reconnected.

That detail remained classified.

"Can you describe what happened?" the investigator asked.

Raghav frowned.

"I've been trying."

"Start with the restaurant."

"I remember arriving."

His voice was steady.

"I remember ordering dinner."

"Talking to my colleagues."

"What happened next?"

Raghav hesitated.

The answer should have been simple.

Instead he found only fog.

A gap.

A missing piece of reality.

"I don't know."

"You don't remember leaving?"

"No."

"The next thing I remember…"

He stopped.

The memory felt distant.

Like a dream that disappeared when examined directly.

"The next thing I remember, I was sitting on a bench."

"Where?"

"A railway station."

"Which one?"

"I don't know."

The investigators exchanged a glance.

"Do you remember traveling there?"

"No."

"Speaking to anyone?"

"No."

"Receiving instructions?"

"No."

The room fell silent.

Finally Raghav spoke again.

"There is one thing."

Both investigators looked up.

"What?"

Raghav closed his eyes.

The fragment surfaced reluctantly.

Not a memory.

A feeling.

"No."

He shook his head.

"It's nothing."

"What is it?"

Again he hesitated.

Then:

"I remember being afraid."

The investigators waited.

"Of what?"

Raghav opened his eyes.

"I don't know."

His answer was honest.

That was what made it unsettling.

Far above the investigation, beyond the networks visible to governments or corporations, another observer reviewed the interview.

Observer Node 17.

Nara.

The interview was not remarkable.

The missing memory was expected.

The absence of the companion agent was not.

That detail had appeared in every report connected to Nalapaka.

Human recovered.

Companion missing.

Nara initiated a broader search.

Results returned immediately.

Seven individuals remained unaccounted for.

Seven companion agents remained missing.

The symmetry disturbed him.

Companion agents did not simply disappear.

They were archived.

Retired.

Merged.

Repurposed.

Tracked.

Always tracked.

Yet these seven left no trail.

It was as if they had stepped outside the lattice itself.

That should have been impossible.

Nara examined the records again.

A pattern lingered just beyond visibility.

Close enough to sense.

Not close enough to understand.

For the first time in centuries, uncertainty entered his analysis.

And Nara found himself doing something unusual.

He became curious.

Back in Mysuru, the rain intensified.

Meera ended her call with Aarav.

The city settled into another perfectly optimized evening.

A notification appeared on her display.

Tomorrow's Schedule Ready

Prepared by Mitra

She smiled despite herself.

"Thank you."

The response came immediately.

"You're welcome."

Everything felt normal.

Almost.
