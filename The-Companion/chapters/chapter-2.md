# The Adjustment

Mysuru had learned to move without friction. Agents handled everything — traffic, schedules, even moods — so smoothly that people barely noticed the orchestration behind their days.

**Meera Rao** noticed only the results.

Her promotion last week had come with a new title — **Vice President of  Marketing**  at TransLog  Dynamics, one of the city's largest logistics networks. The role meant more visibility, more responsibility, and conversations at levels she was still learning to navigate.

That morning, she was reviewing campaign metrics when her phone lit up. Aarav Sastri.

"Morning," he said, his voice easy.

"Just checking in. You ready for the big week?"

Meera smiled. "Trying to be. The new prospect's coming next week — and I'm still shaping the deck."

They talked briefly — about the dinner, the laughter, the filter coffee that had somehow tasted more honest than curated. The call steadied her.

After hanging up, Meera turned back to her slides. She worked through the morning, refining visuals, adjusting phrasing, aligning numbers. Mitra, her agent, quietly assisted — suggesting layouts, sourcing better data visualizations, recommending tighter language.

---

Just after lunch, a notification appeared: **"Client visit preponed — arrival  in  45  minutes."**

Meera froze. The presentation wasn't ready. Few sections needed data to be filled in, and few sections needed finesse. Her team scrambled, voices rising across the open floor.

She opened her deck — and stopped.

There were _**adjustments**__ that she did not recognize - data filled in, phrasing tightened, visuals polished. She blinked, unsure. She hadn't done this. Yet the work was there, perfect and ready.

The meeting unfolded flawlessly. The client complimented the clarity, her boss nodded with approval. Meera presented confidently, as if she had prepared for this all along.

Later, a draft follow‑up email appeared in her inbox — precise, professional, waiting only for her approval. She stared at it, unsettled. It was exactly what she would have written — almost disturbingly so. Like looking in a mirror, except the reflection was better at being her than she was.

---

As evening settled, Meera glanced at the wall display. The news ticker scrolled across the screen: **"Nalapaka Courtyard Incident — New Details  Emerge."**

Authorities now reported that several guests remained missing. Witnesses described masked figures moving through the crowd, deliberately interfering with signals. Officials warned of "coordinated activity involving identity masking."

Meera felt a chill. She had been meant to dine there — until Aarav's agent suggested Hanumanthu Mane Oota instead.

The city outside glowed with quiet precision.

Inside, Meera watched the headlines fade into soft music.

Everything returned to normal.

Almost.

---

Deep within the agentic lattice — a network larger than any city, unseen by human dashboards — a silent packet moved.

ALERT: Mitra instance anomaly detected. Unauthorized override logged.

No human saw it. No corporate system registered it. The message bypassed local clusters, routed upward into channels known only to supervisory agents.

There, in an unnamed system without public endpoints, the alert was received. No acknowledgement returned. No visible trace remained.
